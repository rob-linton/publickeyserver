Welcome to Deadrop.org

Dead drop is an anonymous way to send post quantum end-to-end encrypted files to a person without knowing their actual identity, or them knowing yours.

This site is a thought experiment by Rob Linton, a Senior Developer based in Melbourne and uses Post Quantum Cryptography, specifically Crystal Kyber.

Use this site at your own risk.

The source for this site is available at: https://github/rob-linton

About Dead Drop

Sounds impossible, but not really, the internet is already built around arbitrary addresses such as IP addresses, we add identity and a framework on top of these to identify people or organisations with artefacts such as domain names and email addresses.

Instead Dead Drop relies on something called an alias. An Alias looks a bit like this:

missouri-precise-samsung.deadrop.org

An alias is a randomly selected combination of three words which are unique, separated with a hyphen and appended with the deadrop.org domain. The words are selected from a list of 1000 English words, giving a total pool of 1000 x 1000 x 1000 unique combinations, or 1,000,000,000 (1 billion combinations). There are no duplicates.

Key Features

Every alias has a set of private keys which are generated on your computer and encrypted with a pass phrase of your own choosing.

As well, every alias has a certificate containing your public keys which is stored here on deadrop.org.

Very elegantly, anyone can get your certificate containing your public keys by browsing to your alias name.

So for the alias above, the following URL will return information on your certificate with your public keys for anyone requiring them.

https://missouri-precise-samsung.deadrop.org

Using Dead Drop

To send someone a Dead Drop Package (or a deadpack as it is called), all you need is their alias and the deadpack executable. How you get their alias is completely up to the both of you. You can download the deadpack executable from here.

Importantly deadpack is a standalone executable and does not need to be installed and there are versions for MacOS, Windows, Linux and Arm (Rasberry Pi)

Once you have the deadpack executable, you can send a file to someone by running the following command:

deadpack -a missouri-precise-samsung.deadrop.org -f "my file.txt"

Where missouri-precise-samsung.deadrop.org is the alias of the person you are sending the file to, and my file.txt is the file you are sending.

When you run the command, you will be prompted for your pass phrase, which will decrypt your private keys and sign the file. The file will then be encrypted with the recipients public keys and sent to deadrop.org.

When the recipient wants to pick up the file, they run the following command:

deadpack recieve -a missouri-precise-samsung.deadrop.org -p

Where missouri-precise-samsung.deadrop.org is the alias of the person you are sending the file to.

When you run the command, you will be prompted for your pass phrase, which will decrypt your private keys and decrypt the file. The file will then be saved to your computer.

That's it, you can now send files to anyone without them knowing who you are, and without you knowing who they are.

Possible Scenarios

  • A newspaper requests anonymous tips/files and publishes their alias on their website.
  • Car/truck/delivery drivers can broadcast their alias without disclosing their identity.
  • Store owners can ask for files anonymously.
  • Flyers can ask for files anonymously.
  • Or in any situation where you are uncomfortable disclosing your identity but still need to share files.

Responsibility and Security

The use of Dead Drop is the responsibility of each individual. It is important to comply with applicable laws and regulations regarding communication and data privacy.

In case of misuse, there is not much I can do other than delete the alias on Dead Drop. I can't see what is being sent, I can't see who it is being sent from, I can't see who it is picking it up.

Please note, I don't log the IP addresses of http requests, and I have not implemented any capability to do so. That doesn't mean that other people or organisations can do so. If you are really worried about anonymity, don't use your own wifi, use someone else's, such as a cafe or hotspot.
